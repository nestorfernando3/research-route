# Venue fingerprint

Treat venue choice as a revisable scholarly hypothesis. Record the comparison in `VENUE.md`; store consequential choices in `DECISIONS.md` and link them from `ROUTE.md`.

## Compare candidates and choose provisionally

Begin with two or more plausible candidates. For each, compare current scope, readership, accepted article type, epistemic and methodological range, language, formal limits, review model, access constraints, ethics and AI requirements, and the paper's contribution, materials, method, architecture, and practical constraints. Verify mutable policies from current official instructions when they affect the work and record the date checked.

Propose one **target** and one **fallback**, with evidence, risks, unknowns, and what would trigger reassessment. Obtain researcher approval before recording the provisional choice. A fallback remains a candidate until fingerprinted; a target never licenses distorting evidence or inventing a method the study did not use.

## Build a stratified sample

Sample for information, not convenience. Maintain a matrix with one row per article and columns for citation, URL/identifier, publication date, issue type, topic distance, method or interpretive mode, material/corpus/data, authorship or geographic pattern when relevant, influence signal if used, access level, full text read, article architecture, argument movement, evidence practice, voice/rhetorical stance, boundary revealed, and notes.

Balance the sample across these strata:

- recent ordinary issues, not only special issues;
- close-topic and deliberately distant-topic articles;
- methods, interpretive approaches, and article types represented by the venue;
- established/influential and ordinary contributions where discernible;
- varied authorship, institutions, regions, or languages where relevant to the journal and question;
- boundary cases that reveal what the venue rejects, challenges, or differentiates.

Use editorials, calls, and special issues as additional evidence, never substitutes for representative full articles.

A venue fingerprint is incomplete until at least ten representative articles have been read at full-text level. If access prevents this, record the block and ask the researcher to obtain access or approve another venue; never lower the threshold silently.

Ten is a floor, not an automatic completion signal. Continue until the sample covers the planned strata and new articles have low probability of changing a material venue conclusion. Record remaining unknowns.

## Classify every conclusion

Tag each fingerprint statement with exactly one class: `explicit requirement | strong pattern | tentative inference | unknown | do not imitate | differentiation opportunity`.

- `explicit requirement`: stated in current official venue material;
- `strong pattern`: repeated across the representative sample;
- `tentative inference`: plausible but under-sampled or ambiguous;
- `unknown`: not established;
- `do not imitate`: a pattern that would weaken integrity, voice, or this paper;
- `differentiation opportunity`: a defensible way to extend the conversation while remaining legible to the venue.

Attach article or policy evidence to each conclusion. Describe functions—how articles frame stakes, move arguments, support interpretation, or situate methods—rather than copying sentences or forcing a template. Never imitate an author's prose.

## Decide and reassess

Synthesize:

```markdown
### Venue decision

- target and fallback:
- contribution/readership fit:
- method or interpretive fit:
- evidence and article-type fit:
- formal and policy requirements checked/date:
- differentiation opportunity:
- main mismatch or risk:
- unknowns and access blocks:
- recommendation and alternatives:
- researcher approval:
- reopening condition:
```

Reassess the target and fallback after the contribution stabilizes. Repeat sooner if the thesis, method, corpus, article type, language, access, ethical constraints, or a current venue requirement changes materially. Do not begin venue-driven restructuring until the researcher approves the consequential target decision.
