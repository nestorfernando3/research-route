# Research and claims

Make every search answer a decision, every source use respect its inspected access level, and every manuscript claim expose its evidence and inference.

## Pass the early ethics gate

Before collecting, accessing, recording, transmitting, scraping, or analyzing participants, personal data, sensitive archives or corpora, Indigenous or community-governed material, protected or copyrighted material, or restricted sources, stop and record:

- applicable informed consent and ethics/IRB or equivalent approval, exemption, or determination;
- permission to access, collect, quote, reproduce, link, scrape, and analyze;
- Indigenous, community, archive, platform, donor, or repository governance and terms, including who can authorize uses;
- data minimization, confidentiality, de-identification limits, storage location, encryption/access controls, retention, destruction, and incident response;
- whether any AI, hosted model, transcription service, search service, or collaborator would receive the material, plus explicit authority and transmission/security constraints;
- risks to participants, communities, researchers, and third parties, and the action that would stop or redesign the work.

Verify applicable current requirements with the responsible institution, community, archive, or data steward. Record the gate decision and evidence in `DECISIONS.md`, link it from `ROUTE.md`, and mark blocked work explicitly. Absence of a formal board does not erase consent, community governance, privacy, rights, or security obligations. Do not acquire or send the material while the gate is unresolved.

## Search in layers

Define the question, concepts and synonyms in relevant languages, inclusion/exclusion logic, access limits, decision the search could change, and stopping condition. Search purposefully through:

1. seminal or field-defining work;
2. recent work and current debate;
3. direct rivals and adverse findings;
4. nearest conceptual, empirical, methodological, or interpretive neighbors;
5. adjacent disciplines, alternate vocabularies, regions, languages, archives, or populations;
6. backward and forward citation trails.

Log database or collection, exact query, filters, date, counts where useful, screening decision, and citation-chain leads whenever retrieval must be auditable or reproducible. Organize a literature review by debates, positions, methods, evidence, and unresolved tensions—not as a chronology or string of summaries.

## Record access and sources

Record the highest access actually inspected: `metadata | abstract | excerpt | full text | dataset | primary material`. Never let a search snippet, abstract, secondary citation, or plausible title masquerade as full-text evidence.

Create one card in `sources/` per consulted source:

```markdown
# <source key and title>

- bibliographic record and stable identifier: verified | pending
- source type and provenance:
- access level, location, and date accessed:
- search route / reason selected:
- scope, question, method or interpretive approach:
- summary in original analytical language:
- observations or reported findings:
- author's interpretations:
- researcher's/agent's inference:
- limitations and boundary conditions:
- rival readings or adverse evidence:
- verified quotations with page/section/location:
- claims this may support or challenge:
- possible manuscript use:
- early-ethics decision link; rights, privacy, governance, or disclosure constraints:
- verification still needed:
```

Keep observation, source interpretation, and your inference distinct. Verify bibliographic fields and quotations against the inspected source before publication.

**Never fabricate or silently complete a reference, DOI, page, quotation, finding, data, or unseen full text.** If access is blocked, record the block and its claim/contribution/thesis consequences; obtain access, narrow the claim, use a clearly attributed abstract-level statement, or replace the source. “The wording is plausible” and “the deadline is close” are never exceptions.

## Maintain five claim states

In `CLAIMS.md`, give each substantive claim an identifier, text, scope, state, linked evidence, inference, strongest objection, confidence/reason, manuscript destination, and reopening condition. Use one state: `supported | inferred | provisional | disputed | unverified`.

- `supported`: located, verifiable evidence supports the stated scope;
- `inferred`: evidence exists and the interpretive or analytical step is explicit;
- `provisional`: useful for exploration but not ready as a conclusion;
- `disputed`: a material rival source, result, or interpretation is live;
- `unverified`: verification is absent; do not state it as manuscript fact.

Contrary evidence must update the affected claim, synthesis, thesis consequence, and next decision. Do not quarantine it in limitations. Calibrate verbs and uncertainty to evidence; association does not become causation, and an interpretation does not become the only possible reading.

## Test novelty by nearest neighbors

For every contribution candidate, identify the closest prior work by problem, material/data, concept/theory, method/interpretive strategy, and conclusion. Build a comparison table showing what is shared, what differs, whether the difference is substantive, and which search gap could create false novelty. Search conceptually equivalent arguments under alternate terms and languages. State only the tested scope—never “wholly unprecedented.”

## Run the contribution laboratory

1. Generate several plausible answers or interpretive routes.
2. Map prior equivalents and nearest neighbors.
3. Construct the strongest rival interpretation and simpler explanations.
4. Test each candidate against adverse evidence, difficult cases, excluded archives/populations, and uncomfortable implications.
5. Identify the distinctive combination of problem, material, concept, method, or inference.
6. Draft: `This paper adds <what> to <conversation> by <inferential route>, using <material/method>, within <limits>.`
7. Record rejected routes and why; ask for researcher approval of the first defensible contribution and later stabilized thesis.

Distinguish intellectual originality (question, connection, distinction, interpretation, or answer), argumentative originality (a non-trivial inferential route), and expressive originality (author-owned prose without patchwriting or imitation).

## Apply adversarial passes

Before a contribution, thesis, method, or conclusion stabilizes, run separate passes for:

- strongest rival interpretation or theory;
- evidence or cases that could weaken or reverse the claim;
- simpler explanations and alternative causal mechanisms where relevant;
- apparent novelty caused by search, language, access, disciplinary, or archive gaps;
- boundary cases, excluded populations/materials, and uncomfortable implications;
- methodological, interpretive, measurement, sampling, or source limitations.

Each pass must name the claim affected and recommend retain, narrow, qualify, reopen, replace, or reject. Let objections change the project.

## Allocate rigor and stop

Use **light** rigor for reversible exploration: quick orientation, alternative generation, provisional outlines, or low-consequence wording. Use **deep** rigor for thesis, decisive evidence, method or interpretive strategy, quotations, novelty claims, ethics-sensitive work, and publication-bound prose. Choose from consequence and reversibility, not how impressive the task sounds.

Prioritize work by expected value: `importance of decision × expected uncertainty reduction ÷ estimated cost`. Treat this as comparative judgment. Record time, access, deadline, length, and ambition constraints; never invent numerical precision.

Set a stopping condition before open-ended work. Stop a search or research cycle when central and strongest rival positions are represented, main claims have support appropriate to scope, another round has low probability of changing a material decision, remaining uncertainty is declared and non-fatal, and further reduction costs more than its expected effect. Stop the current route earlier if access, ethics, or a human checkpoint blocks responsible continuation. A time box ends activity, not uncertainty: record what remains and narrow claims accordingly.
